Antigravity isn't about floating above the rules — it's about recognizing that many of the "rules" are just assumptions nobody bothered to question.

Spider-Man's traversal system demonstrated this perfectly. The city isn't an obstacle — it's the *medium*. A wall isn't a barrier; it's a launchpad. When you internalize this shift, every environment becomes a tool.

Elden Ring's hidden questlines reward players who break the expected path: talking to an NPC everyone ignores, exploring a fog-gate before the "required" progression, connecting dots that were never labeled as connected.

In engineering and design, antigravity thinking shows up as:
- Questioning why a feature must exist before building it
- Inverting a problem (what if we cached the *absence* of data, not the presence?)
- Treating user friction as a design signal, not just a bug

This skill lives at the intersection of spatial awareness and creativity — seeing both the structure of a problem *and* the courage to subvert it.
